Who We Are

The Local Marine Spatial Planning (LMSP) - Tanzania initiative is an informal ad hoc group of experts, researchers, practitioners, and professionals dedicated to advancing community-centered marine spatial planning in Tanzania and the Western Indian Ocean region.

Tanzania's coastal waters are among the most biodiverse and economically important marine ecosystems in Africa. From the coral reefs of Zanzibar to the mangrove forests of the Rufiji Delta, these waters support vibrant fishing communities, diverse wildlife, and growing industries like tourism and aquaculture. Yet these resources face mounting pressures‚Äîoverfishing, climate change, habitat loss, and competing uses. The LMSP initiative emerged from the recognition that local communities, working with government and experts, are best positioned to develop sustainable solutions tailored to their unique context.

This diverse composition ensures that LMSP solutions are grounded in both scientific evidence and community wisdom, making them more effective and durable.

What is Local Marine Spatial Planning?

Imagine a fishing village where traditional fishers, conservation experts, government officials, and tourism operators sit down together to map out their coastal waters. They discuss where fishing should happen, where marine ecosystems need protection, where tourism facilities can be developed, and how to manage conflicts between different ocean users. The result is a collaborative plan‚Äîa Marine Spatial Plan‚Äîthat allows all these activities to coexist sustainably. This is the essence of Local Marine Spatial Planning (LMSP).

Local Marine Spatial Planning (LMSP) is a place-based approach to ocean and coastal management that integrates the diverse needs and uses of marine spaces while ensuring sustainability. Rather than top-down regulations imposed from distant capitals, LMSP starts with communities and works upward, ensuring that local voices shape the decisions affecting their waters.

üåä How LMSP Works:

  • üéØ Centers on communities: Places local knowledge and community priorities at the heart of planning. Communities know their waters best.
  • ‚öñÔ∏è Integrates multiple uses: Balances fishing, conservation, aquaculture, tourism, energy, and other activities in harmony rather than conflict.
  • üìä Is science-informed: Uses the best available data and research to support decision-making, combining scientific and traditional knowledge.
  • üîó Connects local and national: Links community-level plans to national MSP frameworks and regional initiatives like those across the Western Indian Ocean.
  • üí™ Enhances stewardship: Builds local capacity and commitment to ocean care and management, creating long-term sustainability.

Through LMSP, communities gain agency in managing their marine resources, governments improve policy coordination, and ecosystems receive better protection. It's a win-win-win approach that has worked in places across the world, and Tanzania is positioned to become a regional leader.

LMSP-Tanzania works across five interconnected areas to advance Local Marine Spatial Planning in Tanzania and the broader Western Indian Ocean region:

üî¨ Research & Knowledge

ü§ù Facilitate Collaboration

We bring together diverse stakeholders‚Äîcoastal communities, government, conservation organizations, private sector, and development partners‚Äîto work collaboratively on marine spatial planning. üìö Share Knowledge

üåç Build Capacity

üîó Connect Scales

Each of these activities reinforces the others. Research informs training. Collaboration generates knowledge to share. Capacity building strengthens communities' ability to participate in collaborative processes. Together, they create momentum for sustainable ocean governance across Tanzania.

Guiding Principles

These principles guide everything we do at LMSP-Tanzania. They represent our collective belief about what makes marine governance effective, fair, and lasting:

üë• Community Stewardship

Coastal communities have the right and responsibility to steward their marine resources. External expertise supports, but communities lead.

üìä Evidence-Based

Planning grounded in scientific research and local ecological knowledge. We trust data, but also respect generations of community wisdom.

üéôÔ∏è Inclusivity

All stakeholders‚Äîfishers, women, youth, government, businesses‚Äîhave a voice in planning processes. No one is excluded or ignored.

‚öñÔ∏è Sustainability

Economic, social, and environmental sustainability are integrated. We don't sacrifice one for the others; we seek solutions that work for all three.

üîÑ Adaptive Management

Plans evolve as environmental and social conditions change. We monitor outcomes and adjust approaches based on what we learn.

‚ö° Equity

Benefits are fairly distributed and vulnerable groups are protected. Prosperity must be shared, and the poorest must benefit most.

ü§ù Collaboration

Success requires working across sectors and scales‚Äîcommunities with government, fisheries with conservation, local with national.

These principles aren't theoretical ideals‚Äîthey emerge from years of experience in the field. They reflect what communities have told us works, what creates lasting change, and what builds trust between groups that might otherwise view each other with suspicion.
