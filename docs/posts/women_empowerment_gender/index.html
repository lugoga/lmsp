Women’s Empowerment and Gender Justice in Marine Resource Management

Article
Exploring women’s roles, challenges, and opportunities in sustainable fisheries and coastal development in Mkuranga and Mkoani
Author

Masumbuko Semba

Published

January 24, 2026

Keywords

women, gender, empowerment, marine resources, fisheries, coastal Tanzania, Mkuranga, Mkoani

Why Women Matter

Women are half of coastal populations in Mkuranga and Mkoani, yet their contributions have been invisible and undervalued. When women are empowered to participate fully in decisions about marine resources, everyone benefits:

When Women Lead - Fish management improves and productivity increases - Household livelihood and food security strengthen - Children’s education and health improve - Communities adapt better to change - Local institutions become more accountable

What Women Do

Fish Processing and Trading: Women control 70-80%+ of dried fish trade. Hundreds work in formal groups, thousands informally. This work is essential for food security but often done in poor conditions without protections.

Seaweed Farming: Women comprise 50%+ of seaweed farmers in Mkoani because it requires little capital, needs flexible scheduling, and provides quick income ($500-1,500/year per farmer).

Fish Capture: Women shore-seine, glean, and fish traps—contributing substantially to household food. Yet this work often goes uncounted in official statistics.

Tourism Services: Women increasingly manage hotels, restaurants, guide tourists, and sell crafts—earning better money than fishing while meeting growing tourism demand.

Barriers Women Face

Women workers face multiple obstacles:

Social and Cultural: Men often control household decisions. Women’s domestic responsibilities limit time for work. Harassment at landing sites and workplaces. Norms say fishing is “male work” even though women fish.

Assets and Access: Land and water rights often controlled by men through inheritance. Credit access difficult without collateral or male relative co-signing. Limited education reduces skills and opportunities.

Markets: Limited market information. Transportation challenges reaching buyers. Price fluctuations hurt profit. Competition from men and other women without fair pricing.

Participation: Fishing organizations dominated by men. Women’s voices don’t carry equal weight in meetings. Leadership positions reserved for men. Consequences of Gender Inequality

For Sustainable Fisheries: Women’s knowledge underutilized, sustainability decisions made without women’s input, resource allocation inefficient, management resistance.

For Food Security: Dietary diversity declines, child nutrition suffers, household food insecurity increases, malnutrition and stunting increase.

For Poverty and Wellbeing: Women’s poverty constrained despite income generation, limited investment in health/education, household vulnerability increases, women’s agency and self-esteem undermined.

For Climate Resilience: Adaptation strategies incomplete without women’s input, maladaptation risks, community adaptation capacity limited, unequal burden of climate impacts.

For Poverty and Livelihood Security

  • Women’s incomes constrained by barriers
  • Household poverty higher than necessary
  • Vulnerability to shocks increases
  • Limited livelihood diversification
  • Economic mobility limited

For Health and Wellbeing

  • Women’s health outcomes decline
  • Work-related health impacts (smoke exposure in processing)
  • Reproductive health impacts
  • Mental health impacts from exclusion
  • Children’s health and education affected

For Community Stability and Resilience

  • Community capacity for adaptation reduced
  • Conflict and social tension increase
  • Community cohesion weakens
  • Democratic governance undermined
  • Adaptation to climate change slower and less effective

Pathways to Gender Equality and Women’s Empowerment

Solutions: Five Pathways to Equality

Economic Empowerment: Create income opportunities (aquaculture, fish processing, tourism), build assets (equipment, credit), include women in savings and microfinance groups. When women earn income, they gain food security, assets, and independence.

Political Empowerment: Reserve positions for women in community organizations. Teach leadership skills. Let women participate equally in planning decisions. When women’s voices are heard, decisions improve.

Legal and Policy Reform: Strengthen women’s property rights. Ensure fisheries policies recognize women’s roles. Protect women from harassment and violence. When laws support women, they’re protected and secure.

Build Skills: Technical training in fish processing, aquaculture, tourism. Business training in planning, budgeting, marketing. Leadership training in advocacy and negotiation. Skilled women earn more and advance faster.

Change Attitudes: Dialogue with men and communities about gender equality. Show women as successful leaders. Engage men as allies for change. When norms shift, equality becomes normal. ## Real Examples

Seaweed Farming (Mkoani): Women started skeptical. Men doubted it would work. Now 5,000+ households with women controlling 50%+ of production. Women’s voices now listened to in planning meetings.

Fish Processing Group (Mkuranga): Women organized, invested in equipment, improved working conditions. Now recognized leaders in community discussions.

Young Women in Tourism: Girls earning good money as hotel staff and guides. Staying in communities instead of migrating to cities. More economic power in their families.

How MSP Supports Gender Equality

Marine spatial planning works better when women are equal partners:

  • Women in planning: Proportional representation on committees. Women’s priorities and concerns heard directly.
  • Plan content: Recognition of women’s fishing roles. Protection of women’s processing work. Fair benefit distribution to women.
  • Monitoring: Women collect and analyze data on gender impacts. Women give feedback on what’s working and what needs adjusting.
  • Accountability: Plans regularly reviewed through gender lens. Adjustments made based on women’s experiences.

Community Members: Support gender equality, participate in women’s groups, advocate for representation, challenge limiting norms, mentor younger women, support women’s activities.

Women Leaders: Build equality movements, develop leadership in others, engage men as allies, advocate for policy change, document successes.

Government: Adopt gender-responsive policies, ensure women’s representation, allocate resources to equality programs, integrate gender in planning, monitor and enforce.

Professionals: Support women’s organizations, facilitate capacity building, document successes and lessons, advocate for policy change, ensure equitable service delivery.

The Path Forward

Women’s empowerment and gender justice are not luxuries—they are essential for: - Sustainable fisheries (women’s knowledge and compliance) - Food security (women prioritize child nutrition) - Poverty reduction (women reinvest in families) - Climate resilience (women’s innovation and adaptation) - Democratic governance (equitable representation and voice) - Human dignity (freedom and choice)

Moving Forward

Mkuranga and Mkoani need gender equality in marine management. Women’s success in seaweed farming and fishing groups shows that with support, women become powerful agents of change.

The path forward: recognize women’s contributions, remove barriers to participation, provide empowerment support, and build institutions where women’s voices matter equally.

Evidence globally is clear: when women are empowered, communities are more resilient, decisions improve, and sustainable management succeeds.

This vision is achievable. Evidence from around the world and from these districts shows that when women are empowered, entire communities benefit—stronger economies, healthier families, more resilient communities, and more sustainable fisheries.

The path forward requires commitment from women and men, communities and government, local and international actors. It requires persistence through resistance and backlash. But the evidence is clear: gender equality and women’s empowerment are essential for sustainable development and thriving communities.
